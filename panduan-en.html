Introduction

Why is Faraid Important?

When a Muslim passes away, the estate left behind cannot simply be distributed according to personal wishes. Allah SWT has established a fair distribution system through Faraid law — each heir receives a share as set out in the Al-Quran.

This system ensures that no heir is disadvantaged, and the estate is distributed fairly based on the responsibilities and family ties between the deceased and their heirs.

This guide walks you through the entire process — from the mandatory steps before calculation, to the method for precisely determining each heir's share.

Priority

Obligations Before Faraid Distribution

The net estate eligible for Faraid is only calculated after all the following obligations are settled, in order of priority. Heirs are only entitled to the balance after all these deductions are complete.

1

Funeral Management Costs

Reasonable expenses for bathing, shrouding, and burying the deceased are taken first — neither excessive nor insufficient.

2

Debts of the Deceased

All debts must be settled before distribution:

  • Debts to Allah — zakat, kaffarah, nazar, hajj
  • Debts to fellow humans — loans, bills, outstanding payments
3

Jointly Acquired Property

Property acquired jointly by husband and wife during marriage. The surviving spouse's share is set aside first — this is not part of the estate, but the spouse's own property.

4

Will (Wasiat)

The deceased may will up to 1/3 of the net estate to a specific party — subject to the condition that a will cannot be made to a Faraid heir without the consent of all heirs.

Heir Categories

Who Are the Entitled Heirs?

Heirs in Islam are divided into two main categories based on the type of entitlement they receive.

Ashab al-Furud — Fixed-Share Heirs

Heirs who receive a fixed share as stated in the Al-Quran. Their share does not change except in certain circumstances.

HeirConditionShare
HusbandNo children1/2
HusbandHas children1/4
WifeNo children1/4
WifeHas children1/8
MotherHas children / 2+ siblings1/6
MotherNo children, <2 siblings1/3
FatherHas sons1/6
DaughterOne, no sons1/2
Daughters2+, no sons2/3

Asabah — Residuary Heirs

Asabah heirs receive the remaining estate after fixed-share heirs take their portions. If there is no remainder, they receive nothing.

HeirNotes
SonAsabah bi nafsihi. Together with daughters, ratio 2:1
FatherBecomes Asabah if there are no sons
GrandfatherIf there is no father
Full BrotherIf there are no children and no father
BaitulmalIf there are no direct heirs

Note: Sons and daughters together are counted as Asabah — a son's share is twice a daughter's share (ratio 2:1).

Method

The Faraid Calculation Flow

Here is the correct order for calculating Faraid — from determining the net estate to distribution among heirs.

1

Determine the Net Estate

Deduct funeral costs, all debts, jointly acquired property, and the will from the gross estate.

2

Identify Entitled Heirs

List all surviving heirs and verify their relationship to the deceased.

3

Assign Fixed Shares

Give each Ashab al-Furud heir their fixed share based on the Faraid table.

4

Check the Total — Apply Aul If Needed

If the total shares exceed 1, apply Aul — proportionally reduce each heir's share.

5

Distribute the Remainder to Asabah

If there is a remainder after fixed shares, it is distributed to Asabah heirs in order of priority.

6

Baitulmal (If No Heirs)

If the deceased has no direct heirs, the entire estate is handed over to the state Baitulmal.

What is Aul?

Aul occurs when the total of all heirs' fixed shares exceeds the value of the available estate. For example, if the total shares add up to 13/12, each heir receives their share out of a denominator of 13 instead of 12.

This way, all heirs still receive their portion — just at a slightly smaller proportional rate. The principle of Aul ensures fairness is maintained even when the estate is limited.
